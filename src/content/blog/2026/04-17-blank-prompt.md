Your whole schooling life you're told what to do. In the classroom the teacher
is trying to get you to grok something. They assign homework to hammer the
points home. In your free time your parents guide you into play. Now many
parents carefully curate a child's friends and in much of the world, arrange
marriage.

As I was growing up I was set on strict behavioral rails by religion. Even the
most subtle tone or word choice that was outside the strict behavioral norms of
the church would earn you a strong talking to.

When I got into college was the first time I used a terminal directly.
Unfortunately it was learning old MD DOS, but the feeling struck me. Its an
experience that all programmers to some extend go through. 

Looking at a completely black screen with a blinking block cursor... What do I
do? What do I type? What _can_ I type? I know all the power and knowledge and
skill I need lives in this terminal, but all I have to go on is a blinking
cursor.

The feeling and thoughts that arise in this moment are interesting, terrifying,
and later awe inspiring. Like floating, free falling, then eventually learning to
fly.

I imagine the first time playing minecraft with no idea what it is would be very
similar. You know there's so much potential, but you have _nothing_ to go off
of. 

In this situation you're forced to engage a different part of the brain. One of
free thinking, curiosity, and exploration. What emerges when you dive in is a
sort of reflection of who you really are.

It's well known among programmers that when working with folks on a team you can
quite quickly tell who wrote what code just from looking at the code. All of the
micro decisions on naming, formatting, tool choice, abstraction level, etc. all
build a reflection of the mind of the person writing the code. If you know the
person you can get into their head while reading and modifying the code to keep
consistency (and sanity) while working with them. This is one of the many
reasons team building is so important on software teams and that thrashing of
people or code or projects is so harmful. A person cannot be plug-and-play and
neither can the team or codebase.

But let me say more about the emergence of self in this open creative space. My
first few programs were bad, but they were mine: a reflection of my mind in the
moment. I also got into music. My music was bad. Many would disagree but I never
felt like I was as good as I should be given how much I practiced. It was bad
because it didn't sound like the people who were good. I was no Charlie Parker
despite studying his work for days practicing every note over and over.
Something about music is incredibly difficult to imitate.

But what I failed to realize is that my music, and my programs (later), were not
actually _bad_. Just different. They reflected me, some people liked that,
others did not. Most don't the code I write that I _really_ love, because they
are not me. For people on the fringes I imagine this is even more difficult.
For folks in the middle of the bell curve many more people will resonate with
your free thinking self expression, but the majority still will not.

But Charlie Parker also thought his music was bad. And at first it was. The
point is this is not new. If we are concerned with what the majority think, we
are all terrible at everything. That's fine, lets get past it.

So what does this have to do with the future of humaity?

In a world of AI we're getting even more structure. We're guided by our
teachers, parents, religion, peers, and now even our computers. Soon it will be
in our phones and in every app and program we use.

There is a boon and a curse here.

The curse is obvious. We lose both our ability and will to think freely and
express ourselves. As opportunity to do so shrinks, when we do have the blinking
cursor experience or play our first musical note, the AI will be right there to
tell us how bad it was. We'll never try again, or we'll look for safeguards to
protect our ego.

The boon is a bit more hidden, but more important. As everyone gets on rails,
what matters? Say AI makes it easy to produce anything you want. Say your job is
to build websites. Now instead of working you can manage a farm of AI to build
the websites for you. Easy! But... why would anyone buy a website from you
anymore when they can just have their AI do it for them? When everything is
free, what has any value? When everyone on rails has everything they think is
good, what do you have left to give the world?

The answer is the same as it's always been, just hidden behind all the societal
pressures, parents, teachers, religious pressure. Under it all, the thing of
most value is and always has been personal free thought and self expression.
Your ability to actualize yourself in the world without care of judgement _is_
the thing you have to give to the world. And in a world of AI, it's even _more_
valuable than ever.

There will always be someone out there better than you at any given thing. But
you are the only person who every is, was, or will every be _you_.

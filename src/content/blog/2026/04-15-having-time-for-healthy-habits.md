High leverage healthy habits:

- Exercise
- Sleep
- Mindful practice
- Diet

"High leverage" means these are required for your brain to work well. Anything
else you do in life requires your brain to work well. Therefore to optimize the
time and attention you spend on _anything_, you must be sure you're doing
these high leverage habits.

When my wife is grumpy I run down the list: did you exercise, did you get enough
sleep, did you practice mindful attention, how has your diet been?

Inevitably she will roll her eyes and say something like, "sure, I can never do
all those things, I simply don't have time in my already overwhelmingly busy
life".

And my answer to that is simple - these habits are a key part of optimizing time
doing _anything_. How can I be so confident in my absolute here? Because these
are how you do it for a human. Other conscious creatures have variations, but
humans universally need these to optimize time/energy/effort of any other
endeavor in life.

To put it simply, it's not that you don't have time to exercise, it's that you
must exercise if you want time to do anything else.

You can think of it another way - if you work a 12 hour day, you're exhausted
but maybe you got a ton of work done. Now say you lack sleep and work a 12 hour
day. How much less work are you getting done because of the constant drag of
lack of sleep? It's more obvious with sleep but it's the same for all the
others. Science backs this up. The brain needs sleep, exercise, some kind of
focused attention, and a balanced diet.

So what is the minimum effective dose of each?

Exercise can be as little as 10 minutes of walking per day.

Sleep varies between people but generally aim for 8 hours and focus on the
quality of sleep. If you have trouble with sleep try no artificial light 1 hour
before you go to sleep and 1 hour after you wake up.

Practice mindfulness 12 min/day. Mindfulness practice can be all sorts of things but it's easiest when doing
while walking or sitting as still as possible. Focus on a single anchor with the
"spotlight" of the mind. Notice when the mind wanders and bring attention back
to the anchor. This is one "rep" of exercising focused attention. Also focus on
the "floodlight" of the mind. Try to pay careful attention to all sensory input
and thought, juggling attention to see where it wanders but keeping broad open
attention. When the mind narrows in on one thing, notice that and bring it back
to open awareness. This is one "rep" of exercising broad attention.

Diet: variety and moderation. That's it. Just eat a reasonable amount of a wide
variety of things. You may have to calibrate "reasonable amount", but typically
people know when they're going overboard.
